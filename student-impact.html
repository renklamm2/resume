Why Individual-Level Matters for SOL Outcomes

State-level SOL pass rates are a building’s scoreboard, but they are built one student at a time. A student who moves from a 380 to a 420 on a Reading SOL didn’t do it on test day — they did it across 30+ weeks of small, specific instructional decisions. The work below is what that looks like up close.

Profile A — Grade 3 Reading, Tier 3

From below-benchmark to on-grade in one cycle

SPIRE · Amplify mCLASS · 20-week cycle

Entry: DIBELS composite well below benchmark; phonemic segmentation and nonsense-word fluency the limiting sub-skills.

Plan: SPIRE Levels 1–2 sequenced to attack segmentation first, then blending, with a daily decoding warm-up and a weekly fluency probe.

Outcome shape: Steady weekly gains, a plateau at week 9 that triggered a scaffold change, and a strong push to benchmark by EOY.

“Three weeks ago you couldn’t read these — today you did all of them.” The student kept that probe in their folder for the rest of the year.
Profile B — Grade 4 Math, Tier 2

Rebuilding fractions without losing computation

Foundations Math · Empower · SOL-aligned strand work

Entry: Solid number sense, weak fractions and multi-step word problems. At risk of failing the Grade 4 Math SOL despite passable classroom work.

Plan: Concrete-representational-abstract sequence on fractions; word-problem “reading” routine borrowed from the reading block.

Outcome shape: Fractions strand jumped from ~25% to ~70% mastery; computation held steady; word-problem accuracy followed once the reading routine clicked.

Profile C — Grade 5 Dual Support

The student who had stopped trying

Reading + Math intervention · behavior plan in lockstep with classroom teacher

Entry: Below grade level in both subjects, low classroom engagement, history of refusing tasks. The academic gap was real but the bigger problem was the belief that effort wouldn’t change anything.

Plan: Short, high-success tasks first, named wins, visible growth chart kept in the student’s folder. Weekly sync with the classroom teacher to keep messaging consistent.

Outcome shape: Skill gains followed the engagement gains, not the other way around. Volunteer hand-raising rose first; reading fluency and math fact recall rose after.

The score gains matter. What I remember is the day this student walked into intervention and asked, before sitting down, “Can I do another one of those probes today?”
Operating Principles That Show Up in Every Profile
Diagnose, then teach

The first 1–2 sessions of any new student are diagnostic. Instruction is wasted on the wrong sub-skill.

Two flat data points = change something

If progress monitoring is flat for two weeks, the plan changes — not the expectation of the student.

Name the win out loud

Growth that the student can see is growth they will work for. I keep visible records and reference them often.

Stay synced with the classroom

Intervention is a wedge, not a silo. Weekly contact with the classroom teacher and Interventionist is non-negotiable.

Protect the relationship

Behavior is information. A student who has stopped trying needs the relationship rebuilt before any curriculum will land.

Document with fidelity

Clean Amplify, VALLS, and DIBELS data is how the building makes good decisions. Sloppy data is worse than no data.
