Why the Work Looks Different Up Close

Aggregate pass rates are a building’s scoreboard, but they are built one learner at a time, across many weeks of small, specific instructional decisions. The generic patterns below describe the shape of what an effective intervention cycle does at the individual level — not any actual student.

Pattern 1 — Tier 3 Reading: Catching the Curve

From well-below-band to within-band over a single cycle

Generic structured-literacy intervention · full intervention cycle

Typical entry: Learner sits well below the typical-range band, with foundational decoding sub-skills the rate-limiter.

Plan shape: Sequenced phonics work targeting the specific blocking sub-skill first, then the next, with frequent low-stakes progress checks.

Pattern of growth: Steady gains, a mid-cycle plateau that triggers a scaffold change, and a strong push toward the typical-range band by the end of the cycle.

The single most reliable lever I’ve found is naming the win out loud: “A few weeks ago this skill was hard. Today it wasn’t.”
Pattern 2 — Tier 2 Math: Rebuilding One Skill Area at a Time

Targeted gaps close without disturbing existing strengths

Generic foundational math intervention · full intervention cycle

Typical entry: Some skill areas already at or near typical-range mastery; one or two foundational areas significantly behind.

Plan shape: Concrete → representational → abstract sequencing on the gap areas; protect existing strengths by continuing maintenance practice.

Pattern of growth: The blocking skill area moves the most, the strong areas hold or slightly improve, and overall problem-solving confidence rises once the gap closes.

Pattern 3 — Engagement First, Skill Follows

The most stubborn cases respond to relationship before curriculum

Generic intervention pattern · engagement and skill tracked together

Typical entry: Skill gap is real, but the bigger problem is a learner who has stopped believing that effort changes outcomes.

Plan shape: Short, high-success tasks first; named, visible wins; a tight feedback loop with the classroom teacher so messaging stays consistent.

Pattern of growth: The engagement curve moves first. The skill curve follows on a delay — rarely the other way around.

A learner who voluntarily asks to keep working is the leading indicator. The score gains arrive a few weeks later.
Operating Principles That Show Up in Every Profile
Diagnose, then teach

The first 1–2 sessions of any new student are diagnostic. Instruction is wasted on the wrong sub-skill.

Two flat data points = change something

If progress monitoring is flat for two weeks, the plan changes — not the expectation of the student.

Name the win out loud

Growth that the student can see is growth they will work for. I keep visible records and reference them often.

Stay synced with the classroom

Intervention is a wedge, not a silo. Weekly contact with the classroom teacher and Interventionist is non-negotiable.

Protect the relationship

Behavior is information. A student who has stopped trying needs the relationship rebuilt before any curriculum will land.

Document with fidelity

Clean Amplify, VALLS, and DIBELS data is how the building makes good decisions. Sloppy data is worse than no data.
